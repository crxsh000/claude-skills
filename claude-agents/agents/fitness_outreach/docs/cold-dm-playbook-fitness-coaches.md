## PART 1: CLICK PSYCHOLOGY - WHY PEOPLE STOP, OPEN, AND REPLY

### The Three Gates Every Cold DM Must Pass

**GATE 1: THE STOP (Preview Text)**
Your message preview competes with 47 other notifications. The recipient is mid-scroll, probably editing a reel or replying to a client. You have roughly 6-8 words visible in the notification preview on mobile. Those words must trigger one of three responses:

- "Wait... how do they know that?" (Pattern recognition / surveillance effect)
- "That is weirdly specific to me right now" (Relevance shock)
- "That is NOT what I expected from a DM" (Pattern interruption)

What does NOT work at this gate: compliments about their content (every agency opens this way), questions about their business (screams sales pitch), any opener that could be sent to 1,000 people without changing a word.

What DOES work: Specificity that feels observed, not researched. Language that mirrors their private thoughts, not their public posts. Openers that sound like a friend texting, not a stranger pitching.

**GATE 2: THE CLICK-OPEN (First 2-3 Lines)**
They clicked. Now they are scanning. They are NOT reading. They are looking for one of two things:

1. Confirmation this is spam (so they can dismiss it and move on)
2. A reason to keep reading (because something feels different)

The first 2-3 lines must feel like a peer talking, not a pitch loading. The mechanics here are:

- Shared experience framing: "I was doing the same thing..." makes them lean in because coaches are starved for peers who understand their actual day-to-day. Their friends think they "just post on Instagram." Other coaches online look like they have it figured out. Someone who describes their ACTUAL experience creates instant trust.

- Incomplete information: The brain cannot tolerate an open loop. If you say "I figured out why my Calendly was empty three weeks straight" you have opened a loop the brain wants closed. They WILL keep reading.

- Status alignment: The DM must position you as a peer or someone slightly ahead, NOT as a service provider. The moment they detect "this person wants to sell me something," the wall goes up. Coaches have been burned by agencies. The antibodies are strong.

**GATE 3: THE REPLY (The CTA)**
Most cold DMs die here because the CTA asks for too much. "Want me to show you how?" feels like agreeing to a sales call. "Can I send you a quick video?" feels like opting into a funnel.

What triggers replies:

- Low-effort responses: Questions that can be answered in 1-3 words. "Does that sound familiar or am I projecting?" only requires "haha yeah" to continue the conversation.
- Curiosity-driven asks: "Want me to send over what I changed?" The reply is just "yeah" or "sure" but now you have permission to continue.
- Identity-based hooks: "Are you still doing everything manually or have you systematized any of it?" This works because coaches who are still manual feel slightly called out, and coaches who have started systematizing want to talk about it. Either way, they reply.

### The Deeper Psychology at Play

**Parasocial Loneliness:** This avatar posts content to thousands but has almost no peers who understand what their day actually looks like. They are lonely in a way they would never admit publicly. A DM that says "I know what your Tuesday mornings actually look like" pierces that loneliness.

**Competence Threat:** They are GREAT coaches. Their clients get results. But they feel incompetent at the business side. Any DM that validates their coaching ability while naming the business struggle earns trust instantly. They are used to people implying they need to be "fixed." They respond to people who say "you are clearly good at what you do, the back-end is just a different skill."

**Agency PTSD:** Many of these coaches have been burned. They paid $2K-$5K to an agency or course that overpromised. They are now hyper-sensitive to anything that sounds like a pitch. Your DM must feel like the OPPOSITE of what burned them. No guarantees, no hype, no "I help coaches do X." Those phrases trigger immediate pattern-matching to past bad experiences.

**The Comparison Wound:** They see coaches with 50K followers posting income screenshots. They feel behind. They wonder if something is wrong with them. A DM that normalizes their experience and reframes it as a systems problem (not a talent problem) is deeply relieving.

## PART 2: SEVEN MESSAGING ANGLES WITH PSYCHOLOGY BREAKDOWNS

---

### ANGLE 1: "THE BACK-END CHAOS MIRROR"
**Target Pain:** Admin overload, being the bottleneck, operational chaos
**Psychology:** This angle works by describing their private reality so accurately that they feel seen. Most people in their life (followers, friends, family) see the polished front-end - the reels, the transformations, the motivational stories. Nobody sees the 11pm spreadsheet updates, the Stripe refund requests, the client who just stopped responding on day 9. When someone describes that private chaos, it creates an instant bond. The unspoken message is: "I am not a stranger. I have been where you are." This is the most reliable angle because it is the most universal pain point for this avatar and the least addressed publicly.

### ANGLE 2: "THE EMPTY CALENDAR ANXIETY"
**Target Pain:** Unpredictable lead flow, refreshing Calendly, feast-or-famine income
**Psychology:** This hits the nerve that keeps them up at night. The empty calendar is not just a business problem - it is an identity threat. When the calendar is empty, they start questioning everything: their content, their offer, their worth. This angle works because it names a feeling they have EVERY morning but never talk about publicly. The taboo nature of it ("successful" coaches should not have empty calendars) makes it powerful when someone else says it first.

### ANGLE 3: "THE SALES AVOIDANCE TRAP"
**Target Pain:** Fear of selling, discomfort with money, avoiding follow-ups
**Psychology:** This is the shame angle. These coaches became coaches because they love training, love transformations, love helping people. They did NOT sign up to be salespeople. But the business requires selling, and their avoidance of it is directly costing them income. They know this. They feel guilty about it. A DM that names this without judgment - that frames it as a design problem rather than a character flaw - gives them permission to engage. The key here is: do not position selling as something they need to "get better at." Position it as something that can be removed from their plate or restructured so it does not feel like selling.

### ANGLE 4: "THE AGENCY BURN / TRUST WOUND"
**Target Pain:** Having been scammed or disappointed by a past agency or course
**Psychology:** This is a differentiation angle. Instead of ignoring the elephant in the room (they have been pitched before and it went badly), you name it directly. This is disarming because every other DM they receive pretends those bad experiences did not happen. By saying "I know you have probably been burned" or telling your own story of being burned, you separate yourself from the category they have learned to distrust. The psychology here is called "objection inoculation" - you raise the objection before they do, which neutralizes it.

### ANGLE 5: "THE PEER WHO FIGURED IT OUT"
**Target Pain:** Comparison, self-doubt, feeling behind, imposter syndrome
**Psychology:** This is the aspirational peer angle. Not a guru. Not an agency. Someone who was in their EXACT position and found the missing piece. This works because coaches do not trust people above them (gurus feel out of touch) or below them (why would I listen?). They trust people who are slightly ahead and recently were where they are. The "I was you 8 months ago" framing is incredibly powerful because it implies: the gap between where you are and where I am is not talent, it is information.

### ANGLE 6: "THE CONTENT TREADMILL ESCAPE"
**Target Pain:** Burnout from constant posting, feeling chained to the algorithm, posting daily but not seeing revenue growth
**Psychology:** This angle reframes a belief they hold deeply: that more content equals more clients. Most coaches in this bracket are posting 5-7 times per week, spending 2-3 hours daily on content, and seeing minimal correlation between posting frequency and actual revenue. They are exhausted but afraid to stop because "what if the algorithm punishes me?" This DM gives them permission to question the treadmill and introduces the idea that the bottleneck is not content volume but what happens AFTER someone engages with the content.

### ANGLE 7: "THE QUIET MONTH CONFESSION"
**Target Pain:** Income volatility, the shame of a bad month, cash flow anxiety
**Psychology:** This is the most emotionally charged angle. A bad month for this avatar is not just a financial event - it is an existential crisis. They start thinking about going back to a 9-5. They feel like a fraud. They cannot tell anyone because their brand is built on success and transformation. A DM that says "January was brutal for me too" or references the specific emotional experience of a down month creates profound connection. The power here is in breaking the fourth wall - acknowledging what everyone in this space experiences but nobody posts about.

## PART 3B: 2ND MESSAGE STRATEGY (AFTER THE HOOK LANDS)

### Why Permission-to-Pitch Fails at Message 2
- Telegraphs the sale before any value is established
- Forces a binary yes/no decision (puts them in judge mode)
- Breaks the casual/curious tone of message #1
- Collapses you back into the "spam DM" category

### Current Hook Performance
- **Hook (msg 1):** "stupid question... are you a coach?"
- **Stats:** 169 sent → 8 opened (4.7%) → 4 replied (50% reply-to-open)
- **Problem isolated to msg 2:** Hook works, but asking permission to pitch kills the thread

---

### SEGMENT A: New Coaches (0-5 clients) — Msg 2 Variants

**Key Insight:** New coaches are NOT worried about scaling or hitting ceilings. Their pain is:
- "I post content but nobody engages or buys" — the content-to-client gap
- "I don't know how to actually get clients" — certified but can't sell
- "I'm doing everything myself and nothing is working" — overwhelm with no results
- "Other coaches seem to have it figured out, what am I missing?" — imposter syndrome
- "I'm spending all this time on social media for zero return" — effort vs. results frustration

#### A1 — Content Frustration (Recommended first test)
```
bet you post more than you'd like to and still feel like nobody sees it lol. that sound about right?
```
- Curiosity: 8/10 | Spam Risk: 1/10
- Why: Universal new-coach pain. Validating, casual, invites venting. Zero sales signal.

#### A2 — The Invisible Expert
```
asking bc I keep seeing new coaches who clearly know their stuff but are basically invisible online. it's the weirdest disconnect
```
- Curiosity: 8/10 | Spam Risk: 2/10
- Why: Compliments expertise while naming the frustration. "Invisible online" stings truthfully.

#### A3 — The Genuine Question
```
cool. honestly just curious—how are you getting clients right now? like is it mostly word of mouth or are you trying to do the whole instagram thing
```
- Curiosity: 7/10 | Spam Risk: 2/10
- Why: Easy to answer, gives you intel. "The whole instagram thing" frames it as the grind it is.

#### A4 — Identity Pain
```
thought so. real talk—do you feel like you spend more time trying to figure out marketing than actually coaching people lol
```
- Curiosity: 7/10 | Spam Risk: 1/10
- Why: They became a coach to coach, not to be a marketer. Names the identity crisis.

#### A5 — Open Loop
```
asking bc I've been talking to a lot of newer coaches and there's this one thing almost all of them are stuck on. you been coaching long?
```
- Curiosity: 9/10 | Spam Risk: 3/10
- Why: Strong open loop ("one thing") + social proof. Slightly higher spam risk but high intrigue.

## PART 4: THE FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE

### Timing and Psychology

Most coaches will not reply to the first message. This is normal. It does not mean they are not interested. It means they are busy, skeptical, or waiting to see if you follow up like a human or like a bot. The follow-up sequence is where most conversations actually start.

**Key principle:** Each follow-up must add new value or a new angle. Never just "bump" or "checking in." Each message should stand alone as interesting even if they never saw the first one.

---

### FOLLOW-UP 1: Send 48-72 hours after initial DM

**The Casual Add-On (Use if initial DM was story-based)**
```
hey - totally fine if that last message was random lol

I just wanted to add one thing because I keep thinking about it: the thing that made the biggest difference for me was not a strategy or a funnel or anything complicated

it was literally just having a simple system that turned "people who watch my stories" into "people who book calls" without me having to manually DM everyone or feel salesy

anyway - not trying to be weird about it. just felt like it might be relevant to you based on what I have seen
```

**The Pattern Interrupt Follow-Up (Use if initial DM was question-based)**
```
I promise I am not going to keep messaging you haha

I just realized my first DM might have sounded like a pitch and that was not the intent. I am literally just a coach who went through the same phase and remember how much I wished someone had shared what actually worked vs what sounds good on a podcast

if you are ever curious about the operational stuff that moved the needle for me I am happy to share. if not, no hard feelings and I will stop being weird in your DMs
```

**The Value-First Drop**
```
last one from me - I actually wrote out the 3 things I changed that took me from $4K months to consistent $12K+ months without ads or an agency

it is genuinely not a pitch - just the actual moves. I can send it over if you want or I will leave you alone either way

figured I would mention it in case you are in a spot where that would matter. either way, good luck with everything - your content is genuinely solid
```

---

### FOLLOW-UP 3: Send 10-14 days after Follow-Up 2 (Final Attempt)

**The Clean Exit**
```
hey - I am going to take the hint haha. no more messages from me

just want to leave this here: if you ever hit a point where the coaching side is great but the business side feels like chaos, feel free to reach back out. I have been there and the fix was simpler than I expected

either way, keep doing your thing - you are clearly good at what you do
```

**Why this works:** The clean exit is disarming. It removes all pressure. It positions you as someone with something valuable who is confident enough to walk away. Paradoxically, this is often the message that gets the reply - because the urgency of "this person is about to stop offering me something" triggers loss aversion.

## PART 5: DOs AND DON'Ts FOR THIS SPECIFIC AVATAR

### DOs

**DO personalize the first line with something specific to their page.** Before sending any template, look at their last 3-5 posts or stories. Reference something specific. "I saw your post about your client Sarah's transformation" is 10x more effective than a generic opener. The templates above should be PRECEDED by a personalization line when possible.

**DO use their language.** This avatar says: "left on seen," "Calendly link," "Stripe dashboard," "check-ins," "12-week program," "discovery call," "no-shows," "ghosted," "content batching," "algorithm." They do NOT say: "sales funnel," "lead magnet," "CRM," "conversion optimization," "ROI," "KPIs." Match their vocabulary exactly.

**DO send DMs during their active hours.** This avatar checks DMs first thing in the morning (7-9am their time zone) and late at night (9-11pm). Avoid mid-day when they are coaching clients. Morning is best because that is when calendar anxiety peaks.

**DO engage with their content for 2-3 days before DMing.** Like a couple posts. Reply to a story. Leave a thoughtful comment. This warms the cold outreach and makes your name familiar before the DM lands. It also moves your DM out of the "requests" folder and into their primary inbox if they follow you back.

**DO keep the first message under 100 words when possible.** Long first DMs feel like sales letters. Short DMs feel like texts from a friend. Save the longer messages for angles that require a story hook (Angles 5 and 7).

**DO position yourself as a peer, not a provider.** Use "I" language about your own experience. Say "I went through this" not "I help people with this." The moment you say "I help coaches do X" you are categorized as a service provider and the walls go up.

**DO make the CTA a question they can answer in 1-5 words.** "Does that sound familiar?" "Is that where you are at?" "Want me to send it over?" These are low-friction. "Would you be open to a 15-minute call to discuss?" is high-friction and will not get replies from this avatar.

**DO be honest about your intent eventually.** Peer framing gets the conversation started, but you need to be transparent about what you offer before it feels manipulative. The goal of the cold DM is to start a conversation, not to close a sale. Transition naturally.

**DO send voice notes as follow-ups when possible.** A 30-60 second voice note feels incredibly personal and almost impossible to ignore. It proves you are a real person, not a bot. This avatar receives dozens of copy-paste DMs. A voice note stands out immediately.

**DO track everything.** Log every DM sent, every reply, every conversation stage. Note which angles get the highest reply rates. This avatar respects systems - and you need one too. Use a simple spreadsheet with columns: Name, Handle, Date Sent, Angle Used, Follow-Up Stage, Reply Y/N, Conversation Status.

---

### DON'Ts

**DO NOT use the word "scale."** This is the single most overused word in coaching DMs. The moment a fitness coach reads "scale" they mentally categorize you as an agency or guru. Use "grow," "build out," or better yet, describe the specific outcome: "fill your calendar" or "stabilize your income."

**DO NOT open with a compliment about their content.** "Love your content!" or "Your page is amazing!" is how every single agency DM starts. It is a red flag, not a relationship builder. If you must compliment, be hyper-specific: "That reel about your client's squat form correction was actually really smart" feels real. "Your content is fire" feels automated.

**DO NOT mention revenue numbers in the first DM.** Saying "I went from $3K to $30K months" sounds like every guru ad on their feed. It triggers skepticism, not curiosity. Let them ask about numbers later.

**DO NOT pitch in the first message.** The goal of the first DM is ONE thing: get a reply. That is it. Not book a call. Not explain your service. Not send a Loom video. Just get two humans talking. Everything else comes later.

**DO NOT send the same template to everyone.** Customize based on what you see on their page. A coach who just posted about losing a client needs a different angle than a coach who just celebrated a client win. Read the room.

**DO NOT follow up more than 3 times.** After three follow-ups with no reply, move on. You can circle back in 60-90 days with a fresh angle, but pestering is the fastest way to get blocked and damage your reputation in a small community. Coaches talk to each other.

**DO NOT send DMs from an account with no content.** Your profile IS your credibility. If a coach clicks your name and sees 3 posts and 200 followers, your DM is dead on arrival. You need a profile that looks like a real person who actually coaches or has coached. Content about the behind-the-scenes of coaching businesses is ideal.

**DO NOT use automation for the initial DM.** This avatar can smell automation. The phrasing, the timing, the lack of personalization. Send every first DM manually. You can use tools to track and organize, but the messages themselves should be typed by a human who looked at the prospect's page.

**DO NOT link anything in the first or second message.** Links in DMs trigger spam filters and trigger the prospect's "this is a funnel" alarm. No Calendly links, no websites, no lead magnets. Just conversation. Links come after trust is established.

**DO NOT use emojis excessively.** One or two is fine and feels natural. Five or more feels like a marketing email. Match the energy of how this avatar actually texts - which is usually minimal punctuation, lowercase, and casual.

**DO NOT DM people who are clearly already at $20K+ months.** The templates above are calibrated for coaches in the $2K-$12K range. A coach who is already crushing it will not resonate with "empty calendar" messaging. Target accurately.

**DO NOT pretend to be something you are not.** If you are not a fitness coach yourself, do not pretend to be one. Adapt the framing to be honest: "I work with coaches on the back-end stuff" or "I built systems for my own coaching business and now I help others." Authenticity is the only sustainable strategy.

## PART 6: EXECUTION NOTES

### Daily Outreach Rhythm

- Research 10-15 prospects per day (check their content, stories, engagement patterns)
- Engage with 5-10 prospects' content (comments, story replies) as a warm-up
- Send 5-8 personalized cold DMs per day
- Send follow-ups to prospects at the appropriate stage
- Log all activity in tracking spreadsheet
- Review reply rates weekly and adjust angles accordingly

### Expected Metrics (Realistic Benchmarks)

- First DM reply rate: 8-15% (good), 15-25% (excellent)
- Follow-up sequence total reply rate: 15-25% (good), 25-40% (excellent)
- Reply-to-conversation rate: 50-70%
- Conversation-to-call rate: 20-35%
- Overall DM-to-call rate: 3-8%

### Angle Testing Framework

Run each angle for a minimum of 20 sends before judging performance. Track:
1. Open rate (if measurable through "seen" status)
2. Reply rate
3. Quality of replies (enthusiastic vs lukewarm)
4. Conversation depth (did it go past 3 messages?)
5. Call bookings generated

Rotate your best 2-3 angles and retire the rest. Refresh templates every 4-6 weeks to avoid message fatigue in overlapping audiences.

### The Conversation After the Reply

Once they reply, your next 2-3 messages should:
1. Validate what they shared ("yeah that is exactly what happened to me")
2. Ask ONE follow-up question to deepen the conversation
3. Offer a specific, small piece of value (not your full offer)
4. Transition to "honestly I think I might be able to help with that - would it be weird to jump on a quick call?"

The transition to a call should feel EARNED, not forced. If the conversation has not reached a point where a call feels natural, keep talking. Patience in DMs converts better than speed.
